Brain Fog Is Real — But The Cause Is Different For Everyone

Brain fog affects millions of people. Whether you're dealing with lingering post-COVID symptoms, perimenopause, ADHD, autoimmune conditions, or simply the aftermath of poor sleep, the experience is the same: mental cloudiness, difficulty concentrating, memory lapses, and a frustrating sense of working below your baseline.

The problem is that doctors often dismiss it. Neurologists order tests that come back normal. Your friends suggest generic advice — "sleep more, eat healthier, exercise" — that doesn't address YOUR specific situation.

The breakthrough: your brain fog has personal triggers. For some people it's a specific food. For others it's sleep architecture, or blood sugar crashes, or stress-induced cortisol spikes, or hormonal fluctuations. The only way to find YOUR pattern is to track systematically and look for correlations.

How It Works in 3 Steps

Find your brain fog triggers through systematic daily tracking.

1

Rate Your Mental Clarity

Each morning and evening, rate your brain fog and mental clarity on a simple scale (1-10). This establishes your baseline and tracks how your clarity fluctuates day to day and week to week.

2

Log Your Potential Triggers

Every day, log the inputs that might affect your clarity: hours slept and quality rating, meals and ingredients, supplements taken, caffeine and alcohol, stress level, exercise, hydration, and other variables. ReactLog integrates with Apple Health to pull in biometric data automatically.

3

Discover Your Correlations

After 2-3 weeks of consistent tracking, patterns emerge. ReactLog's correlation engine shows you which inputs correlate most strongly with your brain fog. You'll see concrete evidence of your personal triggers.

Frequently Asked Questions

What causes brain fog?

↓

Brain fog has many potential causes: poor sleep quality or duration, food sensitivities (gluten, dairy, histamine, eggs), blood sugar crashes, dehydration, nutrient deficiencies (B vitamins, iron, magnesium, vitamin D), chronic stress and elevated cortisol, hormonal changes (menstrual cycle, perimenopause, thyroid), inflammation, medication side effects, screen time and blue light exposure, and post-viral conditions like long COVID. The key insight is that YOUR brain fog likely has specific, personal triggers that may be different from the standard generic advice.

How do I track brain fog effectively?

↓

Effective brain fog tracking involves: (1) Rate your mental clarity and fog level daily on a consistent scale (e.g., 1-10). (2) Log potential triggers that could affect your clarity — sleep hours and quality, meals and foods, supplements, stress levels, exercise, water intake, hormone tracking, caffeine and alcohol consumption. (3) Track this consistently for 2-3 weeks so patterns emerge. (4) Review correlations: which days had the worst fog, and what preceded them? This systematic approach reveals YOUR personal triggers far more effectively than guessing or following generic advice.

Can brain fog tracking actually help me feel clearer?

↓

Yes, significantly. Once you identify your personal triggers through tracking, you can eliminate or manage them intentionally. Many ReactLog users discover that specific foods cause brain fog within hours, or that sleep quality is the dominant factor, or that stress management reduces cognitive cloudiness. By targeting YOUR specific triggers rather than following generic advice, you see real improvement in mental clarity, focus, and cognitive function. The difference is: you're not guessing anymore, you're working with data about your own body.
